## Definition
A framework used to define the boundaries of a research topic or question, particularly in quantitative and medical research.

## How It Works
The framework uses several components to specify the scope of a study:
- **P (Population)**: The group being studied (e.g., firms, students, ethnic minorities, geographic regions).
- **I (Intervention/Exposure)**: The independent variable or the factor being studied (the "X" in a model).
0-**C (Comparison)**: The counterfactual or control group, if applicable.
- **O (Outcome)**: The dependent variable or what is being measured (the "Y" in a model).

Additional refinements can include **T** (Time period) and **D** (Research Design).

## Key Parameters
- Population size and characteristics
- Nature of the intervention/exposure
- Comparability of groups
- Measurability of outcomes

## When To Use
When defining research boundaries, narrowing a topic from a broad "neighborhood" to a specific, executable study design.

## Risks & Pitfalls
- **Over-narrowing**: Defining boundaries so tightly that the topic becomes too peripheral or loses relevance to the broader field (the "Narrowing Paradox").
