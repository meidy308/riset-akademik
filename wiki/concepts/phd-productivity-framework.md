## Definition
A structured approach to managing PhD workloads by moving away from reliance on motivation and toward a multi-layered architectural system of planning.

## How It Works
The framework uses three distinct layers of planning to bridge the gap between long-term thesis goals and daily execution:
1. **North Star (12-week deliverable):** A high-level, written goal that provides direction for a single quarter/season.
2. **Weekly Commitments (3–5 items):** Realistic, precise, and measurable tasks for the current week.
3. **Daily Next Actions:** Tiny, 5-to-10 minute "entry point" tasks designed to overcome procrastination and initiate momentum.

## Key Parameters
- **Planning Horizon:** 12 weeks (North Star) $\rightarrow$ 1 week (Commitments) $\rightarrow$ 1 day (Next Actions).
- **Task Granularity:** High precision for weekly goals; extreme granularity for daily actions.
- **Schedule Structure:** Separation of "Lab/Computational Days" (intensive, distraction-free) and "Desk Days" (writing, reading, admin).

## When To Use
- During periods of research stagnation or "PhD chaos."
- When transitioning between different types of work (e.g., moving from lab experiments to thesis writing).
- As a way to prevent "research drift" by regularizing the weekly reset.

## Risks & Pitfalls
- **Over-commitment:** Trying to achieve more than 5 weekly commitments, leading to failure and demotivation.
- **Vague Goals:** Setting goals like "work on methodology" instead of "write 500 words."
- **Neglecting the Reset:** Skipping the 20-minute weekly reset, which leads to a loss of momentum.
