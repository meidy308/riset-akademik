## Definition
Research drift is the gradual, often unnoticed, misalignment of the components within a research project's "spine" (gap, question, claim, aim, methods). It is characterized by increasing friction, confusion, and loss of motivation despite continued effort.

## How It Works
Drift typically occurs through small, incremental changes rather than sudden breaks. The chain of logic in the PhD spine begins to bend at one or more points, meaning the researcher's efforts no longer directly contribute to addressing the original research gap or proving the intended claim.

## Common Causes
- **External Feedback**: Suggestions from supervisors or colleagues that pull the project in directions inconsistent with the original gap or aim.
- **Shiny Object Syndrome**: Being seduced by promising side ideas or "rabbit holes" that are interesting but not central to the spine.
- **Scope Creep**: Attempting to address too much within a single PhD, leading to an unmanageable and poorly defined thesis aim.
- **Methodological Mismatch**: Adopting new methods (often due to pressure or availability) that are incapable of answering the research question.

## Risks & Pitfalls
- **Burnout**: The psychological toll of working hard without seeing clear progress toward a coherent goal.
- **Unpublishable Results**: Reaching the end of the project only to find the findings do not actually address the identified gap.
- **Project Failure**: The risk of being unable to finish within the allotted time due to an ever-expanding or misaligned scope.
