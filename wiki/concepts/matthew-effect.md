## Definition
A sociological phenomenon in academia where initial success leads to further success, creating an intensifying cycle of advantage for certain researchers or institutions.

## How It Works
Often described as "the rich get richer," this effect manifests through:
- **Grant Acquisition**: Early winners of prestigious grants are more likely to secure subsequent funding.
- **Institutional Halo Effect**: Researchers at top-tier institutions often have easier access to high-impact topics, established networks, and influential supervisors.
- **Citation Advantage**: Highly cited papers attract even more attention, reinforcing the prominence of certain authors and themes.

## Key Parameters
- **Initial Success/Advantage**: The starting point (e.st., a first grant or a prestigious affiliation).
- **Cumulative Advantage**: The mechanism by which success breeds more opportunity.

## When To Use
Understanding this effect is crucial for researchers—especially those at less prominent institutions—to realize that they must actively "short-circuit" the disadvantage through rigorous topic validation (e.g., via the Calibration Test) to ensure their work hits a meaningful debate.

## Risks & Pitfalls
- **Inequality**: It can mask the true quality of research by favoring established names and institutions, potentially overlooking innovative but under-reservingly funded ideas.
