## Definition
The "PhD Spine" is a structural framework consisting of five interdependent elements that must be logically aligned for a research project to remain manageable, coherent, and successful.

## How It Works
A PhD project functions as a chain where each link supports the next. If any link is misaligned, the entire project experiences "drift" or "breakage." The components are:

1.  **The Gap**: The foundational anchor; what is currently missing in the existing literature or field.
2.  **Research Question**: The specific mechanism designed to address and bridge that gap.
3.  **Core Claim/Idea**: The central hypothesis, argument, or finding you intend to demonstrate.
4.  **Thesis Aim**: A realistic, scoped, and deliverable version of the core claim that defines the project's boundaries.
5.  **Methods**: The practical tools, data, and techniques used to test or demonstrate the thesis aim.

## Key Parameters
- **Alignment**: The degree of logical connection between each component.
- **Scope**: The breadth of the research question and thesis aim (must be manageable).
- **Feasibility**: The ability of the chosen methods to actually deliver on the claims made.

## When To Use
Use this framework when evaluating a new research proposal, diagnosing why a current project has stalled, or restructuring a project after significant feedback.

## Risks & Pitfalls
- **Broken Links**: Having a great gap but a research question that doesn't address it.
- **Methodological Mismatch**: Choosing methods that cannot provide the level of evidence required by the core claim (e.g., using descriptive data to try and prove causality).
